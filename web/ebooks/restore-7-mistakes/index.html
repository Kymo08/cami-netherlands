Introduction

Why Most Approaches to Chronic Pain Don't Work

If you are reading this, you have probably tried a few things already. Physiotherapy sessions that helped for a week and then stopped working. Painkillers that masked the sensation but left the problem untouched. Rest — which felt logical but seemed to make things worse over time. Maybe a gym programme that re-injured you within the first month.

You are not imagining it. Those approaches often fail for a very specific reason: they treat the symptom, not the movement pattern that created it.

Chronic musculoskeletal pain — the kind that lasts more than three months — is almost never a simple structural problem. It is a systems problem. It involves how your nervous system interprets threat, how your muscles coordinate (or fail to), how your body has learned to compensate, and how your daily movement habits perpetuate the cycle.

Clinical Note

Research consistently shows that structural findings on MRI (disc bulges, "wear and tear", degenerative changes) do not reliably predict pain or disability. Many people with significant imaging findings have no pain at all. Conversely, people in severe pain often show minimal structural changes. Movement quality and nervous system sensitivity are far stronger predictors of pain experience than structural anatomy.

This guide covers the seven most common movement mistakes I see in clinical practice — patterns that actively perpetuate pain, delay recovery, and reduce quality of life. For each mistake, I will explain the clinical mechanism, the signs to look for, and a practical correction you can start using today.

You do not need to fix all seven at once. Start with the one or two that resonate most, apply the corrections consistently for two to four weeks, and build from there.

01
Fear-Avoidance
Avoiding Movement Because It Hurts

The Mistake

You have learned to protect yourself by avoiding the movements that trigger pain. You modify how you sit, stand, walk, and lift. You stop activities you used to enjoy. The logic seems sound — if movement causes pain, less movement means less pain.

In reality, this strategy almost always makes things worse over time.

The Clinical Mechanism

This pattern is described in kinesiological literature as the Fear-Avoidance Model (Vlaeyen & Linton, 2000). Here is what happens in the body:

When movement triggers pain, the nervous system creates an association: this movement = danger. The brain, trying to protect you, increases its sensitivity to signals from that area — a process called central sensitisation. Over time, it takes less and less stimulus to trigger a pain response.

Simultaneously, avoiding movement leads to tissue deconditioning: muscles weaken, joint cartilage loses its nutrition (which comes from movement-driven fluid circulation), and connective tissue stiffens. Deconditioned tissue is more vulnerable — and more painful.

The result is a self-reinforcing cycle: avoidance → deconditioning → increased sensitivity → more pain with less movement → more avoidance.

Why This Happens

The nervous system cannot distinguish between "movement that causes damage" and "movement that the brain predicts will cause damage." After months or years of pain, the prediction itself becomes the trigger — even when there is no ongoing tissue damage.

Signs You May Be in This Cycle

  • You modify daily activities to avoid pain (avoiding stairs, certain chairs, overhead reaching)
  • You feel anxious or tense before movements that have previously hurt
  • Your pain feels disproportionate to any known injury
  • Pain has persisted for more than 3 months despite rest
  • Your movement repertoire has been shrinking over time

The CAMI Fix — Graded Exposure Protocol

The solution is not to push through pain. It is to systematically re-introduce movement at sub-threshold levels — below what activates your alarm system — and very gradually expand the range over weeks.

The Principle

Dull discomfort at 2–3/10 during movement is acceptable and expected in the early stages. Sharp pain above 4/10 is a signal to reduce range or intensity, not to stop entirely. The goal is gradual, consistent exposure — not heroic effort.

✦ CAMI Exercise Protocol
Graded Range Explorer
Duration: 5 min Frequency: Daily Equipment: None Level: All
  • 1 Identify your comfortable range in the painful area — the range you can move through with zero or minimal discomfort (0–2/10).
  • 2 Perform slow, controlled circles or oscillations within that comfortable range for 30–60 seconds. Breathe continuously — do not hold your breath.
  • 3 Rest for 30 seconds, then repeat 2 more times. 3 sets total, twice per day (morning and evening).
  • 4 After 5–7 days of consistent practice with no increase in pain, expand your range by 10–15%. This is your new baseline.
  • 5 Continue expanding every 5–7 days. Do not rush this process. The nervous system needs repeated safe exposure to update its threat map.
Coaching Cue "Breathe into the movement. Each breath out is a signal to your nervous system that this movement is safe."
02
Sedentary Behaviour
Sitting Too Long Without Active Breaks

The Mistake

You sit for 60, 90, sometimes 120 consecutive minutes without moving. You are focused on work, a meeting, a screen. By the time you stand up, your back aches, your hips feel locked, and your shoulders are tight.

The Clinical Mechanism

Several things happen simultaneously during prolonged sitting:

Gluteal inhibition. The gluteus maximus — your most powerful hip extensor — is mechanically compressed when you sit. Position-dependent inhibition sets in after approximately 20–30 minutes. Weakened glutes force the lumbar spine and hamstrings to compensate for hip extension, increasing load on the posterior chain.

Hip flexor shortening. The psoas and iliacus — deep hip flexors attaching to the lumbar vertebrae — adaptively shorten in prolonged hip flexion. Tight hip flexors pull the pelvis anteriorly, creating lumbar hyperlordosis and compressive loading on the L4–L5 and L5–S1 segments.

Increased intradiscal pressure. Studies using intradiscal pressure sensors (Nachemson, 1981; updated by Sato et al., 1999) consistently show that seated flexion creates the highest spinal loads — higher than standing and only slightly lower than heavy manual lifting.

Reduced spinal nutrition. Intervertebral discs have no direct blood supply. They rely on fluid exchange driven by movement and loading/unloading cycles. Static compression reduces this fluid exchange, impairing disc nutrition and accelerating degenerative changes over years.

Signs You May Be Doing This

  • Back or neck pain that worsens throughout the workday and improves after movement
  • Morning stiffness that takes 15–30 minutes to resolve
  • Hip tightness or restriction that limits your stride when walking
  • Shoulder tension or upper trapezius pain after screen time
  • Feeling "locked up" when you first stand after long sitting

The CAMI Fix — The 30-3 Rule

Every 30 minutes, take 3 minutes of active movement. Not a coffee break. Not scrolling your phone standing up. Deliberate, targeted movement addressing the exact structures compressed by sitting.

✦ CAMI Exercise Protocol
3-Minute Desk Reset Protocol
Duration: 3 min Frequency: Every 30 min Equipment: Chair Level: All
  • 1 Standing Hip Flexor Release (40 sec each side). Step one foot back into a half-kneeling position (or a staggered stance if kneeling is uncomfortable). Posteriorly tilt your pelvis (tuck the tailbone slightly) and hold. Feel the stretch in the front of the rear hip. Breathe slowly.
  • 2 Glute Bridge (10 slow reps). Lie on your back, feet flat, knees bent. Drive through your heels to lift your hips. Squeeze the glutes at the top for 2 seconds. Lower slowly. If floor work isn't possible, do standing single-leg hip extensions holding a wall.
  • 3 Thoracic Extension (10 reps). Sit at the edge of your chair, hands behind your head. Gently extend your upper back over the chair back, opening the chest. Return to neutral. This is the opposite of the rounded forward posture sitting creates.
  • 4 Neck Retraction (10 reps). Keeping your eyes level, draw your chin straight back (not down) — as if making a "double chin." Hold 2 seconds. Release. This decompresses the upper cervical spine and counteracts forward head posture.
  • 5 3 Diaphragmatic Breaths. Inhale slowly for 4 seconds, feeling your belly expand. Hold for 2. Exhale for 6. This resets your autonomic nervous system and reactivates the core stabilisers before you return to sitting.
Coaching Cue "This is not a stretch. This is maintenance. Every 30 minutes that you don't do this, your body is accumulating a debt. This is how you pay it off before it compounds."
03
Mobility Strategy
Relying Only on Static Stretching

The Mistake

You stretch every day. You hold each stretch for 30–60 seconds, you feel the tension release, and you feel better in the moment. But your flexibility never actually improves. Six months later, the same muscles are still tight.

The Clinical Mechanism

Static stretching works on the viscoelastic properties of connective tissue. Applied force temporarily increases tissue compliance — gives the tissue some slack. The problem is that this effect is largely transient. Within 30–60 minutes of finishing the stretch, most of the tissue returns to its baseline length.

Why? Because your nervous system is in charge, not the tissue. If the muscles controlling a joint are not strong enough to manage movement through an increased range, the nervous system will not grant access to that range. It is protecting you.

This is the kinesiological principle: Mobility = Range × Control. Flexibility without neuromuscular control is instability. The nervous system correctly identifies uncontrolled range as dangerous and restricts access to it.

Furthermore, static stretching before exercise has been shown to temporarily reduce force production in stretched muscles (by up to 8–10%) — which is the opposite of what you want before activity.

Important Distinction

Static stretching is not harmful. It has a place — particularly in the cool-down phase and for restoring circulation. The problem is using it as the primary or only mobility strategy, especially when you have chronic pain and restricted movement.

Signs You May Be Doing This

  • You stretch the same areas daily but feel no lasting improvement
  • Your range of motion feels better immediately after stretching but resets quickly
  • You feel unstable or "wobbly" at the end of your available range
  • Stretching feels like it's "pulling on a tight cable" — resistance without release

The CAMI Fix — Active Mobility Training

The most effective way to permanently improve range of motion is to train both the flexibility and the neuromuscular control at the same time — specifically at end range.

The technique is based on PNF (Proprioceptive Neuromuscular Facilitation) principles: move into the stretch, isometrically contract the stretched muscle (tell the nervous system "I'm in control here"), then relax and move further.

✦ CAMI Exercise Protocol
90/90 Hip Active Mobility Drill
Duration: 6 min Frequency: Daily Equipment: Mat Level: Beginner–Intermediate
  • 1 Sit on the floor with both legs bent at 90°: front leg rotated externally (knee and foot pointing to the side), back leg rotated internally (knee to the outside). This is the 90/90 position.
  • 2 Sit tall over your front hip. Feel the external rotation stretch in the front hip and the internal rotation stretch in the back hip. Hold 20 seconds, breathing continuously.
  • 3 PNF contract: Press your front knee gently into the floor (as if trying to push it through) for 7 seconds. This contracts the hip at end-range, which sends a "safe" signal to the nervous system.
  • 4 Relax, then lean forward 2–3° more over the front hip. You should find a small new range available. Hold for 15 seconds.
  • 5 Repeat the contract-relax cycle 3 times per side. Then switch sides by windshield-wipering both legs to the opposite 90/90 position.
Coaching Cue "You are not fighting your muscles. You are negotiating with your nervous system. The isometric contraction is your proof that you are in control. Without it, the nervous system won't let you in."
04
Breathing & Core
Ignoring Your Breathing

The Mistake

You think breathing is automatic and irrelevant to pain. You have never been told that how you breathe is directly linked to how stable your spine is — and therefore, how much it hurts.

The Clinical Mechanism

The diaphragm is not only a breathing muscle. It is part of the deep stabilisation system of the spine — what clinicians call the intrinsic stabilisation cylinder:

The cylinder has a top (diaphragm), front/sides (transversus abdominis — the deepest abdominal layer), a back (multifidus — the deep spinal muscle), and a floor (pelvic floor). These four structures should co-activate before any arm or leg movement to pre-stiffen the lumbar spine and protect the discs.

When breathing is dysfunctional — chronic chest breathing, mouth breathing, or breath-holding under effort — the diaphragm fails to descend properly, intra-abdominal pressure is not generated efficiently, and the whole cylinder underperforms. The result is reduced spinal stability, increased load on passive structures (discs, ligaments, facet joints), and compensatory over-bracing of the superficial muscles (erector spinae, upper trapezius).

Assessment

Place one hand on your chest, one on your belly. Take a normal, relaxed breath. Which hand moves first and most?

Belly rises first and most → good diaphragmatic breathing pattern.
Chest rises first → compensatory chest breathing. Common in people with chronic pain, stress, and sedentary lifestyles. This is the pattern to correct.

Signs You May Have Dysfunctional Breathing

  • Your shoulders rise visibly when you take a deep breath
  • You tend to hold your breath during effort (lifting, carrying, stair climbing)
  • Your upper trapezius (neck-shoulder area) is chronically tight
  • Core exercises feel ineffective despite regular practice
  • You have chronic low back pain despite "strong abs"

The CAMI Fix — 90/90 Breathing Reset

This drill restores diaphragmatic breathing mechanics by using gravity and positioning to create ideal conditions for the diaphragm to work. It also simultaneously activates the transversus abdominis and pelvic floor, re-establishing the full stabilisation cylinder.

✦ CAMI Exercise Protocol
90/90 Breathing Reset
Duration: 5 min Frequency: Morning + after sitting Equipment: Mat, wall or chair Level: All
  • 1 Lie on your back. Place both legs on a chair or against a wall so your hips and knees are both at 90°. This reduces lumbar lordosis and puts the diaphragm in an optimal dome shape for its full excursion.
  • 2 Place one hand on your lower ribcage (not the belly, not the chest — the lower ribs). Inhale slowly through the nose for 4 seconds. You should feel your lower ribs expand outward and sideways — like an umbrella opening in all directions.
  • 3 At the top of the inhale, pause for 2 seconds. Then exhale through pursed lips for 6–8 seconds. As you exhale, gently draw your lower ribs down and in. Feel your abdominals gently engage at the end of the exhale.
  • 4 Repeat for 8–10 breaths. Focus entirely on the quality of each breath — the expansion of the lower ribs, the relaxed descent of the belly, and the gentle engagement at the end of the exhale.
  • 5 Practise this daily for 4–6 weeks to re-establish the pattern. Once automatic in the 90/90 position, progress to practising it while sitting, then standing, then during light exercise.
Coaching Cue "Your first core exercise is not a plank. It is this. If your diaphragm doesn't work, your core doesn't work. Everything else builds on this foundation."

Training Hierarchy
Building Strength Before Establishing Stability

The Mistake

You add resistance to exercises before your joints can properly control the movement. The result: your body finds the path of least resistance, compensation patterns become ingrained, and injury is a matter of when, not if.

The Clinical Mechanism

Movement quality follows a hierarchy. Skipping a level does not save time — it creates structural deficits that will eventually express themselves as injury or pain:

Level 1 — Stability: Can you control the joint through its available range? Without this, the body recruits superficial global muscles to stabilise — muscles designed for movement, not for support. They fatigue, they create compressive loads, and they create the classic "tight but weak" presentation.

Level 2 — Mobility: Do you have the range to execute the movement pattern correctly? Without adequate mobility, you compensate — lumbar hyperextension instead of hip hinging, knee valgus instead of proper squat mechanics, shoulder impingement instead of true overhead press.

Level 3 — Strength: Only once you have stable, mobile joints does adding external load produce training adaptations without injury risk.

Level 4 — Power: Speed under load requires all three prior levels to be established first. Power training with unstable, immobile joints is a common source of acute injury.

Common Pattern in Clinic

"I do yoga three times a week and lift twice a week but my back still hurts." In most cases, this person has acceptable flexibility but poor segmental stability — the deep spinal stabilisers are not activating before movement. Adding flexibility and strength work on top of a stability deficit multiplies the problem.

Stability Screening — Can You Pass These Three Tests?

  • Dead Bug: 4 reps of 10-second holds, lower back flat on the floor throughout. No arch, no breath-hold.
  • Bird Dog: 10 reps each side with no trunk rotation. Hips level, no lateral sway, spine neutral.
  • Single-Leg Balance: 30 seconds each side, eyes open. No excessive sway, no knee collapse.
The CAMI Rule

If you cannot pass all three screening tests with good form, you are not yet ready for progressive strength training. This is not a limitation — it is a starting point. Most people who address their stability deficit see significant pain reduction within 3–4 weeks, before adding any strength work.

✦ CAMI Exercise Protocol
Dead Bug — Spinal Stability Foundation
Duration: 8 min Frequency: 3–4x per week Equipment: Mat Level: Foundation
  • 1 Lie on your back, arms pointing to the ceiling, hips and knees at 90° (tabletop position). Press your lower back completely flat into the floor. This is your reference position — maintain it throughout.
  • 2 Inhale to prepare. As you exhale, simultaneously lower your right arm overhead and extend your left leg, moving them toward the floor but not touching it. Move slowly — 4 seconds down.
  • 3 At the lowest point, hold for 5–10 seconds. Your lower back should still be flat. If it arches, reduce your range of motion — only go as far as you can control.
  • 4 Return to the start position as you inhale. Switch to the left arm and right leg. That is 1 rep. Complete 6–8 reps per side.
  • 5 Master this before progressing to Bird Dog, then to any loaded exercises. Quality over range. A perfect Dead Bug with a small range is more valuable than a sloppy one with full extension.
Coaching Cue "The Dead Bug is not an abdominal exercise. It is a spinal control exercise. If your lower back lifts off the floor, you have exceeded your current stability capacity. Reduce the range."
06
Movement Patterns
Compensating Instead of Correcting

The Mistake

You have found a way to move around the pain and you have stuck with it. Your brain is brilliant at finding workarounds. The problem is that every workaround has a cost — paid by whatever structure is doing the extra work.

The Clinical Mechanism

Compensation patterns are not posture failures. They are intelligent adaptations. The brain is optimising for efficiency and pain avoidance given the current state of the system. But optimised compensation is still compensation — and over thousands of repetitions per day, it accumulates into structural overload.

Forward Head Posture (FHP). For every inch the head translates forward from the ideal alignment over the spine, the effective load on the cervical spine increases by approximately 10 pounds (4.5 kg). A head that sits 3 inches forward — common after years of screen work — applies the equivalent of a 42 lb load on the cervical spine. The suboccipital muscles, upper trapezius, and levator scapulae work chronically to support this load. The result: chronic neck pain, headaches, and shoulder impingement.

Anterior Pelvic Tilt. Tight hip flexors (from prolonged sitting, Mistake #2) pull the anterior pelvis down and forward. This simultaneously creates lumbar hyperlordosis (excessive lower back curve), inhibits the gluteus maximus (which should posteriorly tilt the pelvis), and increases compressive loading on the lumbar facet joints. Classic chronic low back pain presentation.

Dynamic Knee Valgus. During single-leg loading (walking, running, stairs), the knee collapses inward. Usually driven by hip abductor weakness (gluteus medius) or excessive hip internal rotation. Loads the medial collateral ligament, IT band, and patellofemoral joint — a common cause of knee pain in active adults.

Identifying Your Primary Compensation

  • Head forward of shoulders when viewed from the side → Forward Head Posture
  • Lower back curve that does not flatten when you try to stand tall → Anterior Pelvic Tilt
  • Knee(s) that drift inward when you squat or descend stairs → Dynamic Knee Valgus
  • One shoulder consistently higher than the other → Lateral trunk shift / scoliosis pattern
✦ CAMI Exercise Protocol
Wall Angel — Forward Head + Thoracic Mobility Correction
Duration: 5 min Frequency: Daily Equipment: Wall Level: All
  • 1 Stand with your back against a wall, feet 10–15 cm from the baseboard. Press five points of contact into the wall: head, upper back, lower back (a small natural gap is fine), and both shoulder blades.
  • 2 Raise your arms to shoulder height with elbows bent at 90° (goalpost position). Press your elbows and backs of your hands against the wall. If this feels impossible, your thoracic mobility is significantly restricted — start with just the elbows touching.
  • 3 Slowly slide your arms upward along the wall, maintaining all contact points. Only go as high as you can without your lower back or neck compensating (losing contact).
  • 4 Slowly slide back down. 10 repetitions. The goal over weeks: progressively higher range with all contact points maintained.
  • 5 At the end of the set, perform a chin tuck: keeping your head against the wall, draw your chin straight back (not down). Hold 5 seconds, repeat 5 times. This directly corrects forward head position.
Coaching Cue "The wall is your reference. It is showing you where neutral is. Most people discover they have never felt what neutral actually feels like. That's where the correction begins."
07
Load Management
Increasing Load Too Fast

The Mistake

You feel better. Your pain has reduced. Your energy is up. Naturally, you want to do more — more reps, more weight, more sessions. By Thursday you are in more pain than you were before you started.

The Clinical Mechanism

Different biological tissues adapt to loading at dramatically different rates. Failing to respect these timelines is perhaps the most common reason well-intentioned rehabilitation and training programmes fail:

Muscle: Responds and recovers within 48–72 hours. Feeling strong after 3 sessions is real — muscle fibres are genuinely adapting.

Bone: Structural adaptation requires 6–8 weeks of consistent loading.

Tendons: Collagen turnover — the process of breaking down and rebuilding tendon fibres in response to training — takes 8–12 weeks. Tendons that feel fine do not have the structural integrity of tendons that have had 8–12 weeks to adapt.

Ligaments: 12–16 weeks for meaningful structural change.

The result of increasing load faster than your tendons and ligaments can adapt is called enthesiopathy at best and ligament/tendon rupture at worst. The muscles become stronger faster than the structures that attach them — and those attachment points become the failure point.

The 10% Rule

Never increase total training volume (sets × reps × load) by more than 10% per week. This gives tendon and ligament collagen turnover time to keep pace with muscle development. For people with chronic pain or previous injury, use a more conservative 5% rule in the first 8 weeks.

Signs You May Be Increasing Too Fast

  • Pain above 3–4/10 during exercise (not just muscle fatigue)
  • Pain that appears the day after training, not during it (delayed tissue response)
  • Sleep quality declining despite feeling physically fatigued
  • Persistent joint tenderness that does not resolve with a rest day
  • Feeling strong but noticing joint clicking, catching, or instability
✦ CAMI Exercise Protocol
RPE-Based Load Regulation
Duration: Ongoing Application: Every session Equipment: None extra Level: All
  • 1 Use the RPE Scale (Rate of Perceived Exertion) instead of rigid rep targets. After each set, ask: "How hard was that?" on a scale of 1–10.
  • 2 Early rehabilitation phase (weeks 1–4): Target RPE 4–6/10. This feels easy. That is intentional. You are building tendon tolerance, not muscle size.
  • 3 Building phase (weeks 5–12): Target RPE 6–7/10. Challenging but not exhausting. You could do 3–4 more reps if needed.
  • 4 Pain monitoring rule: If pain rises above 3/10 during a set, reduce load or range immediately. If it rises above 5/10, stop the exercise and note which variation triggered it.
  • 5 Weekly progression: Only increase volume or load when you can consistently complete your target sets at or below your target RPE. Never increase more than one variable (sets, reps, or load) in a single week.
Coaching Cue "Training at RPE 5 for 8 weeks and feeling 80% will get you further than training at RPE 9 for 2 weeks and getting injured. Patience is a training variable."
Conclusion

Your Path Forward

These seven mistakes share a common thread: they each reflect a disconnect between what the body needs and what it is being given. Avoidance instead of graded exposure. Static load instead of active movement breaks. Passive stretching instead of controlled mobility. Chest breathing instead of diaphragmatic support. External load before internal stability. Compensation instead of correction. Speed instead of patience.

None of these are character flaws. They are the predictable result of having chronic pain without a clear clinical framework for addressing it. This guide is that framework.

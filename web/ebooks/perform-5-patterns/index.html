Introduction

Why Most Athletes Plateau — and How Movement Patterns Break the Cycle

Most athletes who plateau are not lacking effort. They train consistently, they push hard, they are motivated. What they are typically missing is structural clarity — a framework for understanding which physical capacities need to be developed, in which order, and through which fundamental patterns.

The human body produces movement through a limited number of fundamental mechanical patterns. Every athletic activity — regardless of sport — is a combination of these patterns, performed at varying speeds, loads, and ranges of motion. Developing mastery of the five fundamental patterns is the most direct route to improving performance across any discipline.

Equally important: the five patterns are where most overuse injuries and chronic pain originate. Poor mechanics in the squat pattern load the knee in the wrong plane. Weak posterior chain in the hinge pattern stresses the lumbar spine. Inadequate scapular control in the push/pull patterns creates shoulder impingement. Insufficient core stiffness in the carry pattern generates lateral trunk instability.

The CAMI Perform Principle

Strength and injury prevention are not in tension — they are the same thing. An athlete who is technically sound in all five patterns will be stronger, more powerful, and significantly more injury-resistant than one who trains harder in flawed patterns. Technical excellence is not for elite athletes. It is the fastest route to becoming one.

This guide walks through each pattern: its anatomy, its sport application, the most common errors, and the training protocols we use in the CAMI Perform programme. It is not a complete training programme — it is the conceptual foundation for building one.

Knee-Dominant
The Squat Pattern

What Is the Squat Pattern?

The squat is a bilateral or unilateral knee-dominant movement in which the hips and knees flex simultaneously under load. It is the primary pattern for developing lower body strength and power, and one of the most-used movements in daily life and sport (sitting, standing, jumping, changing direction).

Primary Muscles

Quadriceps (VMO, VL, VI, RF) Gluteus Maximus Gluteus Medius Hamstrings (eccentric) Adductors Gastrocnemius Spinal Erectors (isometric) Transversus Abdominis

Sport Application

The squat pattern drives vertical force production — which transfers directly to jumping height, sprint acceleration off the blocks, and change-of-direction speed. Athletes with strong, technically sound squat patterns consistently show superior acceleration and jumping performance.

The single-leg squat (split squat, Bulgarian split squat, pistol squat) is particularly important for sport because most athletic propulsion is unilateral — you accelerate, land, and change direction on one leg at a time.

The Three Most Common Errors

  • Dynamic knee valgus. The knee(s) cave inward during descent or ascent. Driven by hip abductor weakness (gluteus medius), hip internal rotation, or ankle dorsiflexion restriction. Directly loads the MCL, patellofemoral joint, and medial meniscus.
  • Anterior knee dominance (knees too far forward). Excessive tibial inclination shifts load from the glutes to the quads and increases patellofemoral compressive force. Often caused by poor hip hinge coordination (inability to send hips back) or ankle mobility restriction.
  • Lumbar flexion at depth ("butt wink"). The pelvis posteriorly tilts and the lower back rounds at the bottom of the squat. Causes: limited hip mobility (particularly hip flexion with internal rotation), insufficient ankle dorsiflexion, or premature depth before mobility is established.
Diagnostic Screen

Perform an overhead squat (arms overhead, feet shoulder-width, squat to depth). Observe: do the knees track over the toes? Does the lower back stay neutral at depth? Do the arms stay overhead, or do they fall forward (indicating thoracic stiffness)? Every deviation tells you something specific about mobility or stability deficits.

✦ CAMI Perform Protocol
Goblet Squat — Technical Foundation
Sets: 3–4 Reps: 6–10 Tempo: 3-1-1 Equipment: Dumbbell or kettlebell
  • 1Hold a dumbbell or kettlebell at chest height (goblet position). Feet shoulder-width, toes turned out 15–30°. Brace the core before moving — inhale, create intra-abdominal pressure, then descend.
  • 2Send the hips back and down simultaneously. Think: hips go down and back, not just down. The knees track in line with the 2nd–3rd toe throughout. No caving inward, no excessive forward lean.
  • 3Descend to parallel or slightly below — only as far as lumbar neutrality is maintained. If the lower back rounds, reduce depth. Depth is earned through mobility work, not forced.
  • 4At the bottom, pause for 1 second. Drive the floor away as you stand — think about pushing the ground down, not pulling yourself up. Exhale through effort.
  • 5The goblet position keeps you honest: if the weight drops, your upper back is rounding. Once you can do 8 reps with controlled mechanics, progress to barbell back or front squat.
Coaching Cue "Knees out, chest up, sit between the feet — not in front of them. The goblet weight is your counterbalance. Use it."

02
Hip-Dominant
The Hinge Pattern

What Is the Hinge Pattern?

The hinge is a hip-dominant movement where the torso hinges forward at the hip joint while the spine maintains neutrality. The primary movers are the hip extensors (gluteus maximus, hamstrings). The spine is a rigid lever, not a moving joint.

The hinge is the foundation of explosive power: deadlifts, kettlebell swings, jumping, running acceleration, and sprinting all rely on powerful hip extension through the hinge pattern.

Primary Muscles

Gluteus Maximus Biceps Femoris (long head) Semimembranosus / Semitendinosus Erector Spinae (isometric) Multifidus (isometric) Latissimus Dorsi Glute Medius / Minimus

Sport Application

Hip extension power is the primary driver of sprint speed, jumping height, and change-of-direction explosiveness. EMG research consistently shows that sprinting at maximum velocity is a hamstring-dominant (not quadriceps-dominant) activity — the hamstrings decelerate the swing leg and generate ground contact force.

Athletes with a weak hinge pattern commonly compensate with excessive lumbar extension (lower back takes the load the hips should provide) — the most common mechanism for athletic low back pain.

The Three Most Common Errors

  • Squatting instead of hinging. The athlete bends the knees excessively and "squats down" instead of "hinging back." The hips travel down instead of back. The posterior chain is never loaded under tension. Result: the deadlift becomes a leg press.
  • Lumbar flexion under load. The lower back rounds during the pull, concentrating shear and compressive force on the lumbar discs and facets. Usually caused by inadequate hamstring flexibility for the required hip flexion angle, or failure to brace the core before the lift.
  • Bar (or load) drifting forward. The load moves away from the body during the pull, dramatically increasing the lever arm and lumbar loading. The lat engagement cue ("protect your armpits") is the fix.
Hip Hinge Assessment

Stand facing a wall, 15 cm away. Push your hips backward toward the wall without bending your knees more than 10°. Your hands reach toward the floor, spine stays neutral. Can you touch the wall with your hips while keeping the spine long? If not, you are squatting the hinge.

✦ CAMI Perform Protocol
Romanian Deadlift (RDL) — Posterior Chain Foundation
Sets: 3–4 Reps: 6–8 Tempo: 3-1-1 Equipment: Barbell or dumbbells
  • 1Stand with feet hip-width, barbell at hip height. Brace the core hard — inhale and create pressure in the abdomen (Valsalva for heavier loads). Soft knee bend (15–20°), maintained throughout.
  • 2Push the hips back as if trying to touch a wall behind you. The barbell slides down your thighs — keep it in contact with your body the entire way down. "Bar stays on the body."
  • 3Descend until you feel a strong pull in the hamstrings, or until your lower back begins to round (whichever comes first). Maintain spinal neutrality throughout — the spine does not flex.
  • 4Drive the hips forward to stand. Squeeze the glutes hard at the top — hip extension, not lumbar hyperextension. Your ribs should stay down, not flare up.
  • 5Once you can RDL 1.25× bodyweight for 6 reps with perfect mechanics, progress to conventional deadlift, then to trap bar deadlift or single-leg RDL depending on sport demands.
Coaching Cue "The hinge is not a squat with a barbell. You are loading the hamstrings eccentrically like a slingshot — and releasing that energy through hip extension. The hips drive the lift. The lower back does nothing except stay stiff."

03
Upper Body
The Push Pattern

What Is the Push Pattern?

The push pattern encompasses all movements where force is directed away from the body — horizontally (bench press, push-up) or vertically (overhead press, push press). The primary movers are the pectorals, anterior deltoid, and triceps for horizontal push; the deltoids, upper trapezius, and triceps for vertical push.

Critically, every push pattern requires scapular stability as a foundation. The shoulder joint is inherently unstable by design (ball-and-socket with a very shallow socket). Its stability comes entirely from dynamic muscle control.

Primary Muscles

Pectoralis Major (horizontal) Anterior Deltoid Triceps Brachii Serratus Anterior Medial Deltoid (vertical) Upper Trapezius (vertical) Rotator Cuff (stabilisers)

Sport Application

Horizontal pushing strength correlates with contact sport performance (rugby, football, wrestling). Vertical pressing power drives overhead throwing, volleyball spiking, gymnastics pressing, and serves as a marker of general upper body strength and shoulder health.

The Three Most Common Errors

  • Scapular winging during push-up or bench press. The serratus anterior is not engaging to protract and upwardly rotate the scapula. The medial border of the scapula lifts off the ribcage. This creates impingement risk and indicates insufficient scapular motor control — the prerequisite for all push pattern loading.
  • Elbow flare during bench press. Elbows at 90° to the torso dramatically increases shoulder joint stress. Optimal: elbows at 45–75° from the torso. Athletes who flare the elbows are almost universally targeting pectorals at the expense of shoulder health.
  • Cervical hyperextension during overhead press. The head juts forward as the bar passes the face, creating cervical shear. Usually caused by insufficient thoracic extension or lat tightness limiting true overhead reach without lumbar compensation.
✦ CAMI Perform Protocol
Push-Up Progression with Scapular Control
Sets: 3–4 Reps: 8–15 Tempo: 3-1-1 Equipment: Floor / rings (advanced)
  • 1Scapular push-up first: In a standard plank position, without bending the elbows, protract (spread) the scapulae — push the upper back toward the ceiling. Then retract (pinch together). 10 reps. If you cannot feel this, you need more scapular activation work before loading the push pattern.
  • 2Perform the push-up with elbows at 45° from the torso (not 90°). Lower slowly for 3 seconds. At the bottom, your chest touches — do not rest, reverse immediately with control.
  • 3At the top, actively push the floor away for an extra centimetre — you should feel the serratus anterior engage under the armpit. This prevents winging.
  • 4Progression order: Elevated push-up (hands on bench) → Standard push-up → Tempo push-up (5-1-1) → Loaded push-up → Ring push-up. Only progress when you can do 15 reps with zero scapular winging.
  • 5For barbell pressing: same mechanics apply. Elbows 45–75°, scapulae retracted and depressed on the bench (for bench press) or in a neutral position (for overhead). Brace hard before each rep.
Coaching Cue "Every push is a serratus anterior exercise first. If your scapula is winging, you're pressing on an unstable foundation. Fix the base before adding load."
04
Upper Body
The Pull Pattern

What Is the Pull Pattern?

The pull pattern encompasses all movements where force is directed toward the body — horizontally (rows) or vertically (pull-ups, lat pulldowns). It is the antagonist to the push pattern and is chronically underdeveloped in most training programmes, creating push:pull muscle imbalances that are a primary cause of shoulder impingement and chronic neck pain.

The target ratio for most athletes is 1:1.5 to 1:2 push:pull volume. That is, for every pushing set, you should perform 1.5 to 2 pulling sets. This counteracts the anterior dominance created by most sport activities and daily life.

Primary Muscles

Latissimus Dorsi Rhomboids (Major + Minor) Middle / Lower Trapezius Biceps Brachii Posterior Deltoid Teres Major / Minor Infraspinatus / Subscapularis

Sport Application

Vertical pulling (pull-ups) directly drives climbing, gymnastic performance, swimming, and overhead sport mechanics. Horizontal pulling (rows) is critical for rowing, kayaking, wrestling grip strength, and postural endurance in any athlete who sits or stands for prolonged periods.

The Three Most Common Errors

  • Pulling with the biceps, not the back. The elbows lead the movement and the lat/mid-back muscles never fully engage. The fix: begin every pull by depressing and retracting the scapulae before the elbows bend. "Shoulder blades to back pockets."
  • Shrugging during vertical pulls. The upper trapezius elevates the shoulder girdle during pull-ups or lat pulldowns. This recruits the wrong muscles and compresses the cervical spine. The cue: pull the ribcage toward the bar — not the bar toward the face.
  • Insufficient shoulder external rotation in horizontal rows. The elbows flare wide and the movement becomes a shoulder extension, not a scapular retraction. This primarily fatigues the posterior deltoid without training the rhomboids and mid-trapezius.
✦ CAMI Perform Protocol
Dead Hang + Scapular Pull-Up Progression
Sets: 3–4 Reps: See progression Equipment: Pull-up bar or rings
  • 1Dead Hang (30 seconds): Hang from the bar with a full dead hang. This decompresses the spine and assesses grip and shoulder passive stability. Breathe continuously. This alone is therapeutic for compressed cervical and lumbar spines.
  • 2Scapular Pull-Up (10 reps): From dead hang, without bending the elbows, depress and retract the scapulae — your body rises 3–4 cm. Return to dead hang. This isolates scapular motor control. Must be mastered before full pull-ups.
  • 3Eccentric Pull-Up: Assisted or jumping to the top position, then lower yourself as slowly as possible (5–8 seconds). This builds pulling strength faster than concentric reps and preserves control. Do 5 reps.
  • 4Full Pull-Up: Initiate from scapular depression, then drive the elbows toward the hips as you rise. Chin clears the bar. Full lockout at the bottom between reps (not kipping). Tempo 1-1-3 (up-top-down).
  • 5Progress to weighted pull-ups once you can do 10 clean reps. Add 2.5–5 kg increments. Also rotate in horizontal rows (barbell, dumbbell, or cable) to balance vertical and horizontal pulling volumes.
Coaching Cue "Pull the ribcage to the bar, not the bar to the face. Elbows to hips. Shoulder blades to back pockets. If you can feel it in your traps, you've lost the lat."
05
Loaded Locomotion
The Carry Pattern

What Is the Carry Pattern?

The carry pattern — also called loaded locomotion — involves moving under load while maintaining spinal and core integrity. It is perhaps the most underused pattern in traditional strength programmes, and arguably one of the highest-value exercises for real-world athletic performance and injury prevention.

Variations include: farmer's carry (bilateral), suitcase carry (unilateral, maximum anti-lateral-flexion demand), waiter's carry (overhead), front rack carry, and zercher carry. Each variation stresses the core stabilisation system differently.

Primary Muscles (varies by variation)

Quadratus Lumborum Transversus Abdominis Obliques (anti-lateral) Hip Abductors (gait stability) Trapezius (scapular depression) Forearm Flexors (grip) Gastrocnemius (gait) Gluteus Medius

Why the Carry is Critical for Athletes

All five patterns are ultimately expressed during locomotion — while moving. The carry trains the body to maintain structural integrity under load during walking, which directly transfers to maintaining core stiffness during sprinting, cutting, jumping, and any activity that requires unilateral lower body forces.

The suitcase carry (single-arm) is particularly diagnostic: if the trunk laterally flexes toward the loaded side, it indicates quadratus lumborum and oblique weakness — almost universally associated with low back pain and reduced athletic performance on the same side.

Dan John's Principle

"If it's important, do it every day. If it's not important, don't do it at all." The carry is important. 10–15 minutes of carry variations at the end of every training session is the highest return-on-time-investment exercise for core strength, grip, and athletic durability. Most athletes who add carries consistently report lower back pain reduction within 3–4 weeks.

The Three Most Common Errors

  • Lateral trunk lean toward the load (suitcase carry). The body takes the path of least resistance and tilts toward the weight. This defeats the purpose — the goal is to prevent that lean, training anti-lateral-flexion strength.
  • Shortened stride and shuffle-walking. Athletes take tiny steps, reducing the challenge to the hip abductors and the anti-rotation demand. Walk with a normal, full stride. The greater the single-leg stance phase, the greater the anti-lateral-flexion training stimulus.
  • Shrugging or elevated shoulder on the loaded side. The upper trapezius is compensating for insufficient lat engagement. The cue: pack the shoulder down before picking up the weight and maintain that depression throughout the carry.
✦ CAMI Perform Protocol
Carry Progression — Farmer, Suitcase, Waiter
Duration: 20–40 metres per set Sets: 3–5 Frequency: Every session (finisher) Equipment: Dumbbells, kettlebells, or trap bar
  • 1Farmer's Carry (both arms): Pick up dumbbells at your sides. Pack shoulders down, brace core. Walk 20–30 metres with tall posture, normal stride. Start here — bilateral carry is the foundation. Load: 25–30% bodyweight per hand for beginners; 50–75% per hand for advanced athletes.
  • 2Suitcase Carry (one arm): Same mechanics, one side only. The challenge is preventing lateral flexion toward the loaded side. Walk, actively resisting the lean. Switch sides. Begin with the weaker side first. Load: 30–40% bodyweight.
  • 3Waiter's Carry (overhead): Press one arm overhead (locked elbow, neutral wrist, ribcage down). Walk 20 metres. This adds a thoracic stability and shoulder external rotation demand. Do not arch the lower back to compensate for limited overhead mobility.
  • 4Mixed carry (advanced): Waiter's carry in one hand + suitcase carry in the other. Simultaneously demands anti-lateral-flexion and thoracic stability. This is as close as you can get to simulating athletic demand in a carry variation.
  • 5Track your carry distance and load weekly. The target for general athletic population: farmer's carry for 40 metres with 50% bodyweight per hand, suitcase carry for 20 metres per side with 40% bodyweight — both with zero trunk lean.
Coaching Cue "Walk like you own the room. Tall, confident, no lean. The weight is trying to pull you over. Your job is to refuse. That refusal is the training."
Training Integration

Putting the 5 Patterns Together

The five patterns are not isolated training choices — they are the structural framework for any well-designed programme. A weekly training plan for a general athletic population should include all five patterns, balanced across the push:pull ratio and distributed appropriately across training days for recovery.

The CAMI Perform Load Management Rule

Progress one variable at a time: sets, reps, or load — never all three simultaneously. Use the 10% rule (maximum 10% weekly volume increase) and autoregulate with RPE. Target working sets at RPE 7–8/10 during development phases. Back off to RPE 5–6/10 every fourth week (deload) to allow connective tissue adaptation to catch up to muscle adaptation.

Conclusion

From Patterns to Performance

The five patterns are simple. Mastering them is not. Each pattern rewards years of deliberate practice: progressive loading, technical refinement, and respect for the biological timelines of tissue adaptation.

Athletes who commit to this framework systematically break plateaus, sustain training for longer, and carry fewer injuries into competition and age. Those who chase novelty — constantly switching exercises, chasing soreness, avoiding the hard basics — rarely build the structural foundation that allows real performance.

This guide gives you the framework. What it cannot give you is the individualised assessment, baseline testing, periodised programming, and daily coaching that translates the framework into measurable progress specific to your sport, your training age, and your current limitations.
